# Comprehensive Income Components: Net Income vs. Other Comprehensive Income

## Core Idea

**Comprehensive income** is the total change in equity during a period from non-owner sources. It has two distinct buckets:

1. **Net income (NI)** — the familiar bottom line flowing through the income statement: revenues, expenses, gains, and losses that are *realized or recognized* in the current period under GAAP.
2. **Other comprehensive income (OCI)** — a holding area for specific unrealized or deferred items that GAAP deliberately routes *around* the income statement to avoid distorting periodic earnings.

The relationship is additive:

```
  Net Income
+ Other Comprehensive Income (OCI)
─────────────────────────────────
= Comprehensive Income
```

OCI items accumulate on the balance sheet as **Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income (AOCI)**, a separate component of stockholders' equity.

## The Four Standard OCI Categories

| Mnemonic | Item |
|----------|------|
| **F**oreign | Foreign currency translation adjustments |
| **U**nrealized | Unrealized gains/losses on AFS debt securities |
| **P**ension | Pension/OPEB prior service cost & actuarial gains/losses |
| **D**erivatives | Effective portion of qualifying cash-flow hedge gains/losses |

Memory aid: **FUPD** — *Four Unusual Parking Destinations* for items not yet "earned" in the traditional sense.

## Worked Example

Assume a company reports:

```
Operating revenues          $500,000
Operating expenses         (380,000)
                           ─────────
  Net Income                $120,000   ← hits income statement

OCI items (net of tax):
  Unrealized loss on AFS     (8,000)
  Pension actuarial loss     (5,000)
                           ─────────
  Total OCI                 (13,000)   ← bypasses income statement
                           ─────────
Comprehensive Income        $107,000
```

Net income is $120,000; comprehensive income is only $107,000. A reader relying solely on net income would overstate economic performance by $13,000.

## Why Deterministic Reference Matters

Because OCI items follow rigid, closed-form classification rules under ASC 220, there is no judgment about *whether* an item belongs in OCI — only *which* of the four categories applies. A deterministic reference ensures candidates apply the exact statutory list without improvising, preventing misclassification errors on simulations where partial credit hinges on correct equity presentation.

## Prereqs

- **Basic financial statement structure** — understanding the income statement, balance sheet, and statement of stockholders' equity
- **Stockholders' equity components** — distinguishing paid-in capital, retained earnings, and AOCI
- **Realization principle** — knowing when gains and losses are considered realized vs. unrealized
- **Available-for-sale (AFS) vs. trading securities classification** — foundational to understanding why certain investment gains bypass net income
