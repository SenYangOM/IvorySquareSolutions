# Impairment of Long-Lived Assets

## Core Idea

A long-lived asset is **impaired** when its carrying amount (book value) can no longer be recovered through future use or sale. Under U.S. GAAP (ASC 360), impairment testing for assets *held and used* follows a strict two-step sequence: a **recoverability test** first, then a **measurement of loss** only if that test fails. This ordered approach prevents unnecessary write-downs while ensuring the balance sheet never overstates an asset's economic value.

---

## The Two-Step Process

### Step 1 — Recoverability Test (Trigger Check)

Compare the asset's **carrying amount** to the **sum of undiscounted future net cash flows** expected from the asset's use and eventual disposal.

```
Carrying Amount   vs.   Sum of Undiscounted Future Cash Flows
   $500,000       vs.          $420,000
   
   $500,000 > $420,000  →  FAILS test → proceed to Step 2
```

If carrying amount ≤ undiscounted cash flows, **no impairment is recorded** — stop here.

### Step 2 — Measure the Impairment Loss

Write the asset down to its **fair value**. The loss equals:

```
Impairment Loss = Carrying Amount − Fair Value
               = $500,000 − $350,000
               = $150,000  (recognized on the income statement)
```

The new carrying amount ($350,000) becomes the asset's **revised cost basis**; subsequent recovery of value is **not permitted** under GAAP for assets held and used.

---

## Assets Held for Sale

Once management commits to a plan of sale, the asset is reclassified and measured at the **lower of carrying amount or fair value less costs to sell** — the two-step recoverability test no longer applies. Depreciation also ceases at reclassification.

---

## Why a Deterministic Reference Matters

Impairment calculations are closed-form: given carrying amount, undiscounted cash flows, and fair value, the loss is arithmetically fixed. Yet exam questions frequently embed traps — using *discounted* cash flows in Step 1 (incorrect) or allowing value recovery (not permitted). A precise, step-ordered reference eliminates ambiguity and prevents the model from blending IFRS rules (which use a one-step discounted approach) with GAAP rules, a known failure mode.

---

## Prereqs

- **Depreciation and carrying amount** — understanding how book value accumulates over time
- **Fair value measurement (ASC 820)** — how fair value is determined for Step 2
- **Asset classification (held and used vs. held for sale)** — determines which impairment model applies
- **Revenue and expense recognition** — impairment losses flow through the income statement
